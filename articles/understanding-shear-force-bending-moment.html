Understanding Shear Force and Bending Moment Diagrams in Beam Analysis

📝 Er. P. Durga Prasad 📅 June 5, 2026 ⏱️ 12 min read

Shear Force Diagrams (SFD) and Bending Moment Diagrams (BMD) are fundamental tools in structural engineering. Every beam, column, and frame that supports loads develops internal forces — and understanding these forces is essential for safe, efficient structural design. Whether you are a civil engineering student learning the basics or a practicing structural engineer verifying designs, mastering SFD and BMD analysis is non-negotiable.

What Are Internal Forces in Beams?

When external loads act on a beam — such as the weight of a concrete slab, furniture, people, or wind forces — the beam must resist these loads to maintain equilibrium. It does so by developing internal forces at every cross-section along its length. These internal forces can be categorized into three types:

  • Shear Force (V): The internal force acting perpendicular to the longitudinal axis of the beam at any given cross-section. It represents the tendency of one part of the beam to slide vertically relative to the adjacent part.
  • Bending Moment (M): The internal moment that causes the beam to bend or flex. It represents the rotational tendency at a cross-section due to external forces.
  • Axial Force (N): The internal force acting along the longitudinal axis, relevant in frames and inclined members but typically zero in horizontal beams with vertical loads.

The Shear Force Diagram plots the variation of shear force along the beam length, while the Bending Moment Diagram plots the variation of bending moment. Together, they provide a complete picture of how a beam responds to loading.

Sign Conventions: Sagging vs. Hogging

One of the most common sources of confusion for engineering students is the sign convention. BeamMaster AI follows the standard structural engineering convention used in most textbooks and professional practice:

Shear Force Sign Convention

  • Positive Shear (+V): When the resultant of forces to the left of a section acts upward, or equivalently, when the right portion tends to move downward relative to the left portion.
  • Negative Shear (−V): When the resultant of forces to the left acts downward.

Bending Moment Sign Convention

  • Positive Moment (Sagging): The beam bends concave upward, creating a U-shape. The bottom fibers are in tension and the top fibers are in compression. This is the typical condition in simply supported beams under gravity loads.
  • Negative Moment (Hogging): The beam bends convex upward, creating an inverted U-shape. The top fibers are in tension. This commonly occurs at the supports of continuous beams or in cantilever beams.
Understanding which fibers are in tension is critical for reinforced concrete design — steel reinforcement must be placed in the tension zone to resist bending.

Mathematical Relationships Between Load, Shear, and Moment

Three fundamental differential relationships connect the distributed load intensity w(x), shear force V(x), and bending moment M(x) along a beam:

Key Differential Relationships

1. dV/dx = −w(x) — The rate of change of shear force equals the negative of the load intensity.

2. dM/dx = V(x) — The rate of change of bending moment equals the shear force at that section.

3. d²M/dx² = −w(x) — The second derivative of bending moment equals the negative load intensity.

These relationships have powerful practical implications:

  • Where the shear force is zero, the bending moment reaches a maximum or minimum value. This is the critical section for flexural design.
  • A concentrated point load causes an instantaneous jump (discontinuity) in the shear force diagram and a sharp change in slope of the bending moment diagram.
  • A uniformly distributed load (UDL) produces a linearly varying shear force and a parabolically varying bending moment.
  • A concentrated moment applied at a point causes a step jump in the BMD while the SFD remains unaffected at that point.

Example: Simply Supported Beam with UDL

Consider a simply supported beam of span length L subjected to a uniformly distributed load of intensity w kN/m over its entire length. This is one of the most common loading scenarios in building structures.

Step 1: Calculate Support Reactions

By symmetry, the vertical reactions at both supports are equal:

R_A = R_B = wL/2

Step 2: Shear Force at Any Section x

Taking a section at distance x from the left support and considering equilibrium of the left segment:

V(x) = R_A − w·x = wL/2 − w·x

The SFD is linear, starting at +wL/2 at the left support and decreasing to −wL/2 at the right support. The shear force is zero at the midspan (x = L/2).

Step 3: Bending Moment at Any Section x

Taking moments about the cut section:

M(x) = R_A·x − w·x²/2 = (wL/2)·x − w·x²/2

This is a parabolic curve. The maximum bending moment occurs at midspan where V = 0:

M_max = wL²/8

This formula — wL²/8 — is one of the most frequently used expressions in structural engineering. It appears in building codes, design guides, and preliminary sizing calculations worldwide.

Cantilever Beam Analysis

A cantilever beam is fixed at one end and free at the other. Under a point load P at the free end:

  • The fixed support provides both a vertical reaction R = P and a fixed-end moment M = P·L.
  • The SFD is constant at −P along the entire length (negative because the shear acts downward on the left face).
  • The BMD is linear, varying from −P·L at the fixed end to zero at the free end. The entire beam experiences hogging (negative moment).

In cantilever beams, the maximum shear and maximum moment both occur at the fixed support — making the support region the most critical section for design.

Practical Applications in Structural Design

SFD and BMD are not merely academic exercises. They directly inform critical design decisions:

  • Flexural Reinforcement: The maximum bending moment determines the required area of tensile steel reinforcement in RCC beams per IS 456 or ACI 318.
  • Shear Reinforcement: The maximum shear force determines stirrup spacing and diameter. Shear failures in concrete are brittle and catastrophic, making this design step critical.
  • Deflection Control: The bending moment distribution is integrated twice to obtain the deflection curve, which must stay within serviceability limits (typically L/250 for floors).
  • Steel Beam Design: The peak bending moment determines the required plastic section modulus Z_p for steel beams, while shear force governs web thickness requirements.

Key Takeaways

  • SFD and BMD are essential for understanding how beams resist external loads through internal forces.
  • The maximum bending moment occurs where the shear force is zero.
  • For a simply supported beam with UDL: V_max = wL/2 and M_max = wL²/8.
  • Sign conventions must be applied consistently — sagging is positive, hogging is negative.
  • These diagrams directly inform the design of reinforcement, stirrups, and section sizing.
